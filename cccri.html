Overview

A community deserved better than what they had

CCCRI (Community Church of Christ — Rhode Island) is a bilingual church serving both English and Chinese-speaking communities. Over time, their digital presence became a patchwork: multiple websites, dozens of disconnected pages, and a navigation system that required calling the church office just to find basic information.

In a single week, I led a complete end-to-end redesign — from discovery and persona development through sitemap architecture, design, and front-end implementation — delivering a modern, bilingual, mobile-first platform that serves real people in the pews and behind screens.

"We originally used engineers and designers to create the first version of the site. As time grew, responsibility became that of non-technical volunteers. Multiple people in charge leads to conflicts in what should be delivered and what is an acceptable outcome."

— CCCRI Stakeholder

🌎

🗺️

Problem / Challenge

Four cracks running through the foundation

The existing site wasn't just aesthetically dated — it was structurally broken. For a church whose job is to be welcoming and accessible, the digital front door was locked, confusing, and invisible on mobile.

🧭
High Navigation Friction
No clear information hierarchy. Navigation didn't work on mobile. Key content — service times, ministries, contact — was buried behind expandable sections and orphaned pages.
📵
Broken Mobile Experience
The majority of visitors browse on phones. The existing site was designed for desktop only, rendering menus inaccessible and layouts broken on smaller screens.
🔤
Disconnected Bilingual Access
English and Chinese content existed in entirely separate, inconsistent structures. Chinese-speaking community members had to hunt for translations with no guarantee of parity.
📂
80+ Pages, Zero Clarity
Content was duplicated across pages, managed by multiple non-technical volunteers with conflicting ideas of what "done" meant. No single source of truth existed for anything.

Goals

What success actually looked like

🎯
Consolidate & Simplify
Reduce 80+ pages to 8 core sections. Every piece of content needs a clear home, and every navigation path needs to work on the first try.
📲
Mobile-First UX
Design for a phone, then scale up. Prioritize finger-friendly targets, thumb-reach navigation, and fast loading — because most visitors arrive on mobile.
🌐
Unified Bilingual Experience
Build English and Simplified Chinese into a single, consistent structure — not two separate sites. Create parity so the language a user speaks doesn't determine what they can access.
📈
Scalable for Non-Technical Owners
The site needs to survive long-term in the hands of volunteers. The new architecture had to be simple enough that anyone could maintain it without breaking the experience.

Users & Context

Designing for real people in the pews

Two very different people needed to succeed on this site. One is arriving for the first time, skeptical, browsing on her phone. The other has been attending for years and just needs to know what's happening this Sunday. The redesign had to work for both.

"I want to know when services are and what to expect before I walk through that door."

Sara Martinez
Age 34 · Teacher & Business Owner · Mobile-First User
Frustrations with the old site
  • Couldn't find event information consistently
  • Too many pages, no clear starting point
  • Left when confused — called the church directly
  • No mobile-friendly experience
How the redesign helps
  • 8-page structure makes entry intuitive
  • Service times and location surfaced immediately
  • Clear CTAs guide her from landing to connection

"I just want to quickly check what's happening this week — I've been coming here for 20 years."

David Li
Age 52 · Construction Supervisor · Desktop + Occasional Mobile
Frustrations with the old site
  • Information buried — too many clicks per task
  • Inconsistent structure across pages
  • Bilingual content not clearly accessible

How the redesign helps
  • Consolidated pages remove recurring confusion
  • Announcements clearly visible on load
  • Unified bilingual experience removes friction

Process

Five phases. One week. Zero shortcuts.

01
Discovery
Full Site Audit
Mapped all 80+ existing pages, documented navigation failures, performed a heuristic evaluation of usability issues, and analyzed mobile vs. desktop experience gaps.
02
Strategy
Architecture & Personas
Developed two primary personas from stakeholder interviews. Rebuilt the sitemap from the ground up — 8 core pages, 2 secondary categories for ministries and fellowships.
03
Design
Mobile-First in Figma
Designed every page starting at 375px. New color system, typography, and component library. Kept the Donate button persistently visible — a critical requirement from leadership.
04
Build
HTML / CSS / JS + AI Assist
Implemented the design in clean, semantic HTML and CSS. Used ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude for code generation, debugging, and copy refinement — accelerating delivery without sacrificing quality.
05
Delivery
Donated & Deployed
Delivered the site as a community donation. Provided documentation and a handoff guide for the volunteer team to maintain the site going forward.

Key Tradeoffs Made

↓
Depth for Clarity — Reduced total content to surface essentials. Not every ministry gets its own page; the most important information gets the most real estate.
→
Edge Cases Deprioritized — Designed for the top two user journeys. Advanced use cases were deferred to a future iteration based on real usage data.
⤴
Bilingual Doubles Content — Accepted that representing two full languages creates more content overhead in exchange for genuine community inclusion.

Key Solutions

The decisions that moved the needle

🗂️
Rebuilt Information Architecture
Consolidated 80+ pages into 8 core sections. Grouped related content logically. Added secondary pages only for Fellowship and Ministry groups — everything else lives on the main pages.
📍
Persistent Donate & Nav CTA
The Donate button stays fixed in the navigation on every page, on every device. No hunting. A core organizational priority made impossible to miss.
🌎
Integrated Bilingual Architecture
English and Chinese content live in the same unified page structure. No separate sites, no separate navigation. Language is a toggle, not a fork in the road.
📣
Visible Announcement Cards
Upcoming events and weekly updates now live on the homepage in scannable cards — visible on first load, no scrolling required for returning members like David.
🎨
Modern Visual Identity
Introduced a cohesive color system, improved typography hierarchy, and a welcoming visual language — bringing the church's digital presence in line with its community character.
⚙️
Volunteer-Maintainable Structure
Every design decision was made with non-technical maintainers in mind. Clean HTML structure, no framework dependencies, and a full handoff guide to protect the investment long-term.

The redesign removed the primary friction points that caused visitors to call the church office for information they should have found online in under 60 seconds. By surfacing service times, ministries, and contact information on the first scroll, the site now functions as the welcoming front door it was always meant to be.

Critically, the bilingual integration removed a long-standing barrier for Chinese-speaking community members, who previously navigated an entirely separate and often outdated set of pages.

Delivered as a community donation, the project established a scalable foundation that the church can build on without needing a developer on call. The cleaned architecture and volunteer-friendly documentation mean the investment is protected for the long term.

The project also served as a proof-of-concept for AI-assisted development — using Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini strategically across the sprint to multiply output without sacrificing judgment or craft.

Reflection

What I learned building this

Key Learning
Less is almost always more
Reducing content rarely means losing value. On this project, removing 72 pages didn't diminish the site — it finally let the important content breathe and be found.
Key Learning
IA is the invisible foundation
No amount of visual polish fixes broken information architecture. Getting the sitemap right before touching Figma was the most impactful decision I made on this project.
Key Learning
Mobile-first forces discipline
Designing at 375px first forced hard prioritization choices — what earns space on a small screen? That constraint produced a better desktop experience as a byproduct.
What I'd Do Next
User testing with real members
Conduct moderated testing sessions with Sara and David archetypes to validate assumptions and find friction points that only real usage reveals.
What I'd Do Next
Add analytics tracking
Implement basic event tracking to understand which pages visitors actually use, where they drop off, and whether the 8-page structure is mapping to real behavior.
What I'd Do Next
Iterate on bilingual UX
The current bilingual system is functional but could be more elegant. I'd explore inline toggle patterns and test language-switching behavior with actual Chinese-speaking users.
